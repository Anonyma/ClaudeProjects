Wallace Stevens
1. "Thirteen Ways of Looking at a Blackbird" — Thirteen short numbered sections, each a different angle on the same image. It's almost like a product designer iterating on a concept. Quick, imagistic, structurally inventive.
2. "The Snow Man" — A single sentence stretched across 15 lines about perception and nothingness. Deceptively simple, rewards rereading.
________________


Wisława Szymborska
1. "Possibilities" — A list poem about preferring small, specific things over grand abstractions. Very readable, quietly devastating.
2. "The Three Oddest Words" — Three tiny stanzas, almost aphoristic. Plays with how language undermines itself. Takes 30 seconds to read, stays with you.
________________


Philip Larkin
1. "This Be The Verse" — His most famous. Eight lines, blunt, darkly hilarious about generational damage. You'll know immediately if he's for you.
2. "Aubade" — Longer, heavier. About waking at 4am and confronting death without religious comfort. Unflinching but somehow not bleak.
________________


Frank O'Hara
1. "Having a Coke with You" — A love poem disguised as casual conversation. Compares being with someone to looking at art. Light touch, real feeling.
2. "The Day Lady Died" — About hearing of Billie Holiday's death. The whole poem is errands and lunch until the gut-punch ending.
________________


Anne Carson
1. "Short Talks" — Not one poem but a series of prose fragments. "On Rain," "On Waterproofing," etc. Strange, funny, aphoristic. Start anywhere.
2. "The Glass Essay" — Much longer, autobiographical, about a breakup intertwined with reading Emily Brontë. For if you want to commit.
________________


Start with Szymborska's "Possibilities" or Larkin's "This Be The Verse"—both short, both immediately tell you if the sensibility fits.
