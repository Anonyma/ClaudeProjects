**Q1:** D - Versailles was designed as a gilded cage to keep the nobility close and under control, effectively domesticating the most dangerous people in France.

**Q2:** B - 1661 is the year Louis XIV took personal control after the death of his chief minister, marking the start of his absolute rule.

**Q3:** B - In 1682, Versailles officially became the seat of government and the court, forcing the nobility to move there.

**Q4:** B - Louis XIV used a system of weaponized etiquette to keep the nobility occupied with status symbols, preventing them from plotting against him.

**Q5:** B - Louis XIV bled the nobility dry by enforcing strict dress codes and high-stakes gambling, making them financially dependent on him.

**Q6:** B - The lever and coucher ceremonies were elaborate rituals surrounding the king's rising and retiring, involving many courtiers and emphasizing status.

**Q7:** B - The Affair of the Diamond Necklace was a scandal that involved a con artist, a gullible cardinal, and Marie Antoinette, who was wrongly implicated.

**Q8:** B - Versailles was incredibly crowded and lacked proper sanitation, leading to unpleasant odors and a chaotic environment.

**Q9:** B - The Versailles glide was a method of walking used to avoid stepping on the long trains of gowns or tripping on the polished floors.
